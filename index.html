Research

Working Papers

Managing a Conflict - Alternative Dispute Resolution in Legal Contests (with Benjamin Balzer)

Abstract: We analyze optimal alternative dispute resolution (ADR) by a third party mediating between two litigants facing each other in court. Formal litigation serves as the outside option if ADR fails. Mediator's objective is to minimize the number of trials.

Litigation is modeled as an all-pay contest in which the two contestants need to provide evidence to convince the court. Contestants differ only in their cost of providing evidence. Thus, the mediator's only screening device is to sometimes fail ADR and send both contestants back to court.

We provide a link between litigant's behavior in ADR and in litigation by showing that the information revealed in ADR has an influence on behavior in litigation. We show that ADR and litigation should not be treated as separated strategic situations but as a one two-staged game. We derive the optimal information structure the mediator induces and show that optimal ADR always reduces the probability of litigation by more than 50%. The cost effect on remaining trials is ambigous.

We use a mechanism design approach in which the mediator solves a two-step problem: (1) it derives the optimal information structure that fulfills incentive compatibility, and (2) scales it such that everyone participates.

Persuasion, Pandering, and Sequential Proposal

Abstract: In this paper, I look at the features of a model in which an informed sender can propose a project to an uninformed receiver. The receiver can accept or reject the projects implementation. If the receiver rejects, the sender can propose a different project (if available) to the receiver, which, in turn, may be accepted or rejected. Overall, only one project can be implemented. Both players share an interest within-project realization. Across projects, preferences are not aligned.

For the case of two projects, I show existence of a robust mixed strategy equilibrium. In it, the sender panders to the unconditionally receiver-preferred project. Increasing the number of periods leads to a second class of equilibria. In this class the sender signals through waiting. The shortest "waiting equilibrium" corresponds in many ways to the mixed strategy equilibrium. Discounting and non-availability play an important role in the evaluation of the equilibria.

As an application I consider a firm that needs clearance of a proposed merger by an anti-trust authority. Both players prefer higher synergies. Post merger the authority prefers high competition, the firm prefers low. Merger realization are private information of the firm.
